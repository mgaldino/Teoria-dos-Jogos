0.1 Game Theory Model of Regime Change

0.2 Introdução

We examine a strategic framework where citizens must decided if they should oppose an authoritarian regime to initiate a transition towards democracy. The model highlights the role of income-based incentives and the costs associated with participation in the movement against the regime.

0.3 The Game

The society comprises \(N\) individuals, segmented into three income-based classes: \(N_{\text{poor}}\), \(N_{\text{middle}}\), and \(N_{\text{rich}}\), representing poor, middle-income, and rich citizens respectively, where \(N = N_{\text{poor}} + N_{\text{middle}} + N_{\text{rich}}\).

Income disparity exists between the classes, not within. Each citizen’s income, denoted by \(y_i\), is identical within a class. The total income of the society is normalized to 1.

Under authoritarian rule, the affluent benefit from reduced taxation, \(\tau_a\). Conversely, a democratic regime would impose higher taxes, \(\tau_d\), to facilitate wealth redistribution. Taxation is proportionate to individual income.

Opting to contest the regime incurs a cost, \(\mu\), for each participant. The challenge only succeeds if the number of dissenting citizens reaches or surpasses a threshold \(k\).

Each citizen faces a binary choice: to challenge (\(C\)) or not challenge (\(NC\)) the regime.

0.4 Payoffs

The utility of each citizen hinges on their action, the collective actions, and the resultant political regime:

\[\begin{align*} \text{Payoff under Democracy: } U_i^{D,C} &= y_i(1 - \tau_d) + \frac{\tau_d}{N} - \mu \text{, for challengers} \\ \text{Payoff under Democracy: } U_i^{D,NC} &= y_i(1 - \tau_d) + \frac{\tau_d}{N} \text{, for non-challengers} \\ \text{Payoff under Authoritarianism: } U_i^{A,NC} &= y_i(1 - \tau_a) \text{, for non-challengers}\\ \text{Payoff under Authoritarianism: } U_i^{A,C} &= y_i(1 - \tau_a) - \mu \text{, for challengers} \end{align*}\]

0.5 Best Response Functions

The best response for each citizen depends on the strategies of others and the expected outcomes: \[\begin{align*} BR_{\text{poor}}(s_{-i}) &= \begin{cases} C & \text{if } \mu < y_{\text{poor}}(\tau_d - \tau_a) + \frac{\tau_d}{N}, \\ NC & \text{otherwise}, \end{cases} \\ BR_{\text{middle}}(s_{-i}) &= \begin{cases} C & \text{if } \mu < y_{\text{middle}}(\tau_d - \tau_a) + \frac{\tau_d}{N}, \\ NC & \text{otherwise}, \end{cases} \\ BR_{\text{rich}}(s_{-i}) &= \begin{cases} C & \text{if } \mu < y_{\text{rich}}(\tau_d - \tau_a) + \frac{\tau_d}{N}, \text{ and expecting } \sum_{j \neq i} h_j \geq k - 1,\\ NC & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} \end{align*}\]

Given the threshold \(k\) for a successful transition, a Nash Equilibrium requires that no individual citizen has an incentive to unilaterally change their strategy, considering the collective outcome of their actions.

0.6 Analysis

We can see that challenging is a dominates strategy for all rich citizens in this game.
